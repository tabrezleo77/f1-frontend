A one-stop strategy relies on tyre preservation and track position. Drivers run longer stints on harder compounds, accepting slower pace to avoid extra pit loss.

Example: Lewis Hamilton won the Russian Grand Prix 2015 using a one-stop, controlling tyre wear while rivals needed additional stops.

The two-stop strategy balances speed and tyre life, allowing drivers to push harder on fresher tyres. It is the most common modern F1 approach.

Example: Max Verstappen won the French Grand Prix 2021 by switching to a faster two-stop while rivals stayed on one.

Three-stop strategies are aggressive and pace-focused, sacrificing pit time for maximum grip. They work only when tyre degradation is extreme.

Example: Michael Schumacher won the French Grand Prix 2004 using a four-stint strategy, pushing qualifying-level laps.

An undercut involves pitting earlier than rivals to exploit the pace of fresh tyres. Timing and traffic prediction must be perfect.

Example: Sebastian Vettel won the Singapore Grand Prix 2019 by undercutting his teammate and controlling the race.

The overcut keeps a driver out longer while rivals pit, using clear air or strong tyre warm-up to gain time.

Example: Fernando Alonso won the European Grand Prix 2012 by extending stints and capitalizing after others pitted.

Safety car pit stops reduce pit loss and allow cheap tyre changes. Entire races can be decided by perfect timing.

Example: Daniel Ricciardo won the Chinese Grand Prix 2018 after pitting under a safety car and overtaking rivals.
